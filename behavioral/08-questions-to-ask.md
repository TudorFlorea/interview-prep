Asking thoughtful questions:

- **Demonstrates preparation** and research

## ðŸ’¼ Role & Expectations Questions

### For Hiring Manager or Team Lead

**Q1: What does success look like in this role in the first 30, 60, and 90 days?**
- Shows you're thinking about impact timeline
- Reveals expectations and priorities
- Helps you assess if it matches your strengths

**Q2: What are the biggest challenges facing the team right now?**
- Shows you want to contribute meaningfully
- Reveals what you'd actually be working on
- Assesses if challenges excite or concern you

**Q3: What's the biggest technical or product challenge this team will face in the next 6-12 months?**
- Demonstrates strategic thinking
- Reveals roadmap and future direction
- Helps assess if it aligns with your interests

**Q4: How does this role fit into the broader organization?**
- Shows you think about context
- Reveals team's importance and autonomy
- Assesses collaboration needs

**Q5: What qualities or skills would make someone truly exceptional in this role vs. just successful?**
- Shows ambition beyond baseline
- Reveals what they truly value
- Helps you understand the bar

## ðŸ‘¥ Team & Culture Questions

**Q6: Can you describe the team structure and how collaboration typically works?**
- Assesses team dynamics
- Reveals autonomy vs structure
- Helps understand communication patterns

**Q7: What's the team's approach to work-life balance?**
- **Variations**:
  - "What are typical working hours?"
  - "How does the team handle on-call or urgent issues?"
  - "What's the meeting culture like?"

**Q8: How would you describe the team's engineering culture?**
- Reveals values (speed vs quality, innovation vs stability)
- Assesses alignment with your preferences
- Shows you care about culture fit

**Q9: What do you enjoy most about working on this team?**
- Personal question that builds rapport
- Gets authentic insights
- Reveals genuine team strengths

**Q10: What's something that surprised you (positively or negatively) about working here?**
- Gets honest feedback
- Reveals hidden aspects of culture
- Shows you want the real picture

**Q11: How does the team handle disagreements or conflicting technical opinions?**
- Assesses psychological safety
- Reveals decision-making process
- Shows you value healthy debate

## ðŸŽ¯ Strategic Question Selection

### How Many Questions to Prepare

- **Prepare**: 8-10 questions total
- **Ask**: 2-4 questions per interviewer
- **Save**: The most important ones for hiring manager/final rounds

### Matching Questions to Interviewers

| Interviewer Type | Best Questions | Avoid |
|------------------|---------------|-------|
| **Junior Engineer** | Tech stack, day-to-day work, code review, team culture | Strategy, compensation, career growth |
| **Senior Engineer** | Technical practices, architecture decisions, technical debt | Business strategy, funding |
| **Engineering Manager** | Role expectations, growth opportunities, team dynamics | Low-level technical details |
| **Product Manager** | Product roadmap, cross-functional collaboration, metrics | Engineering-specific practices |
| **Director/VP** | Company strategy, organizational goals, future direction | Day-to-day technical details |
| **Recruiter** | Timeline, process next steps, team structure | Deep technical or strategic questions |

## âš ï¸ Questions to Avoid

### âŒ Red Flags

**DON'T Ask**:
- "What does your company do?" (Shows you didn't research)
- "How much PTO do I get?" (First interview is too early)
- "Can I work from home?" (Ask after offer, or if remote is standard)
- "How soon can I get promoted?" (Sounds entitled)
- "Do you do background checks?" (Raises concerns)
- "When can I leave early?" (Shows lack of commitment)
- Questions already answered on their website or in the job description

### âš ï¸ Timing Matters

**Save for Later** (after you have an offer):
- Compensation details
- Specific PTO/benefits questions
- Remote work policies (unless fully remote company)
- Start date flexibility
- Negotiation topics

**First Interview (Recruiter Screen)**:
- Process and timeline questions
- High-level role questions
- Team structure basics

**Technical Rounds**:
- Technical practices and stack
- Day-to-day work
- Team collaboration

**Final Round (Hiring Manager/Leadership)**:
- Strategic questions
- Growth opportunities
- Company direction

## ðŸ’¡ Making Your Questions Stand Out

### 1. Build on the Conversation

**Weak**:
> "What's the tech stack?"

**Strong**:
> "You mentioned earlier that the team is working on real-time features. How are you handling the state synchronization challenges? Are you using websockets, or something like CRDTs?"

### 2. Show You've Done Research

**Weak**:
> "What's your product?"

**Strong**:
> "I read your engineering blog post about migrating to microservices. Now that you're 6 months in, what's been the most surprising benefit or challenge of that migration?"

### 3. Demonstrate Strategic Thinking

**Weak**:
> "What will I work on?"

**Strong**:
> "Given that the company is focusing on enterprise clients in 2026, how is the team balancing building enterprise features with maintaining the self-serve product that got you here?"

### 4. Ask Follow-Up Questions

Don't just check boxesâ€”engage in dialogue:

**Initial**: "What's the team's biggest challenge?"

**Follow-up**: "How long has that been a challenge? What's prevented solving it so far?"

## ðŸŽ­ Practice Techniques

### 1. Prepare Question Clusters

Group related questions you can ask based on the conversation:

**If they mention rapid growth**:
- How is the team scaling?
- What processes are you putting in place?
- How do you maintain quality during fast growth?

**If they mention technical debt**:
- How does the team prioritize paying it down?
- What's your approach to balancing new features and tech debt?
- Can you share an example of a recent refactoring project?

### 2. Natural Delivery

- Don't read from a list
- Weave questions into natural conversation
- React to what they're saying
- Show genuine curiosity, not just checking boxes

### 3. Take Notes

- Write down interesting points during their answers
- Shows you're engaged and taking this seriously
- Helps you remember key information for later rounds
- Enables thoughtful follow-up questions

## ðŸ”„ Adapting Questions by Company Type

### For FAANG Companies

**Focus on**:
- Scale challenges (millions/billions of users)
- Engineering culture and practices
- Impact and scope
- Learning from senior engineers

**Example**:
> "Amazon operates at massive scale. What's the most interesting challenge the team has faced around scaling, and how did you approach it?"

### For Startups

**Focus on**:
- Product-market fit
- Growth trajectory
- Role flexibility
- Impact potential
- Runway and funding

**Example**:
> "As an early-stage company, roles often evolve quickly. How have you seen this role or team change over the past year, and where do you see it going?"

### For Mid-Size Companies

**Focus on**:
- Growth stage challenges
- Process maturity
- Career paths
- Engineering culture evolution

**Example**:
> "You're at an interesting growth stageâ€”past startup chaos but before BigCo bureaucracy. How do you maintain startup agility while building more structure?"
